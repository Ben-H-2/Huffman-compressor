The old lighthouse stood at the edge of the cliff, its white paint peeling
after decades of facing the harsh sea wind. Every evening, the keeper
climbed the narrow spiral staircase to light the lamp, a ritual she had
performed for thirty years without missing a single night. Ships passing
in the distance relied on that steady beam to find their way through
the rocky channel below. She often wondered about the people aboard
those vessels, where they were going, and whether they ever looked back
at the small light on the hill and thought of the person who kept it burning.
The work was quiet and mostly thankless, but she found a strange comfort
in its rhythm, the same way a heartbeat goes unnoticed until you stop
to listen for it. On clear nights she could see for miles, the dark water
stretching out until it met an equally dark sky, the two separated only
by a thin line of stars.
